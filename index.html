01 / 03 Halio Sonic · HRM RevitaWave · Static · Meta · US / AU / UK
The avatar

Female, 40-55, HHI $125K+. She is research-oriented and already spending money on her skin: clinic appointments, facials, professional LED treatments. Her psychological driver is control and maintenance of vitality, not transformation. She views any device at $300+ as a capital investment. The fear is not price it is wasting money on something unproven.

The thinking

Most ads in the account were built to communicate clinical quality. That is a product-attribute hook it only reaches people already searching for LED devices. Tiny TAM.

The reframe: she is not price-resistant, she is already spending the money. Anchor against her existing clinic spend, not the product price. Loss aversion is a stronger motivator than gain framing. "Clinic: $3,600+/year vs This: $383" is not a price claim. It is a cancellation. The purchase becomes a decision to stop a bleed, not start a new spend.

Why the hook stops the scroll

She recognises the $3,600 figure immediately. It is her actual number. The ad does not introduce a new problem. It names one she is already living and shows her the exit.

Angle why it sells

Save $3,000+ vs clinic treatments. She is already spending the money this stops the bleed. Loss aversion is the conversion driver.

Concept how it looks

Clean product shot with a split price comparison: clinic cost vs device price. The visual does the math for her before she reads a word.

It worked. 2.97x ROAS aggregated across adsets $14,000 revenue on $4,714 spend. $124 CPP on a $324 AOV product. 38 purchases, 291K impressions. The same loss-aversion frame transferred to all three SKUs ($189, $324, $383) with consistent performance because the anchor is always the clinic cost, never the device price.

02 / 03 Halio Sonic · PureGlow LED Mask · Static · Meta · US / AU / UK
The avatar

Same Clinical Pragmatist, female 40-55, but at a different funnel moment. She has moved past awareness into active comparison mode: Reddit threads, YouTube reviews, two or three tabs open. Her core pain is not "should I do this?" It is "which one is actually worth it and will not waste my money?" She has been burned by cheap gadgets before. Trust and proof are the deciding variables.

The thinking

Most competitor ads attack directly or shout "we're better." They signal inauthenticity and hard selling. Customers get skeptical and scroll past. This ad does not sell it hands her the evaluation criteria she does not know she needs.

4 wavelengths vs 2. Featherlight 93g flexible silicone vs heavy 330g rigid shell. Built-in eye-safe diffusion vs exposed LEDs. RF technology heating deeper layers vs surface-level LED only. She reads it like a checklist. By the time she finishes, her own logic has eliminated the alternative. The ad never pushed she closed the sale herself.

Why the hook stops the scroll

"Not all red light masks are built the same" validates the fear she is already carrying: that she will pick the wrong one. It meets her in research mode and promises to resolve it.

Angle why it sells

Ours is more advanced. She is already comparing give her the right criteria and her own logic closes the sale. The angle intercepts the research moment, not the awareness moment.

Concept how it looks

Side-by-side spec comparison chart: wavelengths, weight, eye safety, water resistance. She reads it like a checklist. The ad never has to push.

It worked. 3.00x ROAS $7,688 revenue on $2,562 spend. 24 purchases, $107 CPP. More importantly it validated the hypothesis: the same customer needs different creative at different funnel stages. A cost-reframe ad and a comparison ad run simultaneously to the same avatar one closes consideration, one closes comparison.

03 / 03 Raffini Swim · Linda One-Piece · Native Story Ad · Meta · Vietnam
The avatar

Female, 20-32, body-conscious, content-native. She discovers and validates through Instagram. From 597 DM conversations analyzed (12,625 messages): 54% mentioned fit before price, 35% of purchase intent was driven by Instagram content quality. Her core desire is not "a swimsuit" it is wanting to feel attractive and look good on camera. She has bought from Shopee before and been disappointed. She needs peer recommendation, not brand claims.

The thinking

A product-forward ad ("premium Vietnamese swimwear, quality materials") only reaches people already searching the category. Tiny TAM. The real desire body confidence and being camera-ready has a massive TAM: every body-conscious woman, not just swimwear shoppers.

The Q&A Story format is a pattern interrupt: it looks like organic content, not an ad. She is in passive scroll mode the format meets her before her ad-blindness filter activates. The hook uses her exact language from real DM conversations, not brand copywriting. The solution lands as peer recommendation she does not feel sold to, she feels like she found an answer.

Why the hook stops the scroll

She has felt that exact frustration and never seen it named this bluntly in an ad.

Angle why it sells

Finally a swimsuit that actually flatters. The angle is the desire she has been chasing and failing to find not the product, not the brand. That desire has a massive TAM. A swimwear ad does not.

Concept how it looks

Screenshot-style UGC Q&A recommendation. Looks like organic Story content. The hook is written in her exact language from real DM conversations she feels like she found the answer, not like she was shown an ad.

8.3% CTR vs 4.51% for the control static in the same account. The Q&A native format nearly doubled click-through across 125,665 impressions. Raffini sells through Shopee, TikTok Shop, and Instagram DMs rather than a DTC site Vietnam consumers purchase through social channels, not direct-to-site. CTR is the right metric for this campaign objective.

That is how I think through every ad. Avatar first. Where they are mentally. What belief needs to shift. Then the mechanism. Then the format.

The same principles transfer directly to your categories. Loss aversion works for supplements: she is already wasting money on products that do not work. TAM reframing works for sleep: do not sell a sleep aid, sell the feeling of waking up without an alarm. The customer psychology is the constant. The category is just context.
